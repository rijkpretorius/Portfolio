The R-squared value of 0.603 indicates that approximately 60.3% of the variation in exam scores can be explained by hours studied, suggesting a moderately strong relationship. To test whether hours studied is a statistically significant predictor at the 5% significance level, we compare the calculated t-value of 17.35 with the critical value of 1.97. Since 17.35 > 1.97, we reject the null hypothesis and conclude that hours studied is a useful predictor of exam score.

Therefore we will continue to determine a regression equation to predict exam score using hours studied.

Finding the equation

In[15]:
X = df[["hours_studied"]]   # 2D array
y = df["exam_score"].values

lr = LinearRegression()
lr.fit(X, y)

b0 = lr.intercept_
b1 = lr.coef_[0]

print(f"Regression equation: exam_score = {b0:.4f} + {b1:.4f} * hours_studied")

Regression equation: exam_score = 23.6184 + 1.6341 * hours_studied

Conlcusion

The regression equation developed in this analysis demonstrates that hours studied is a statistically significant predictor of exam scores. With an R-squared value of 0.603, the model explains just over 60% of the variation in exam performance, suggesting that study time plays an important role in determining outcomes. While this equation can be used to predict exam scores with reasonable accuracy, it does not capture all the factors that influence performance. Other variables such as prior knowledge, study methods, and exam conditions may also contribute to exam results. Nonetheless, the analysis confirms that increased study hours are strongly associated with higher exam scores, and the regression model provides a useful tool for making predictions within the scope of the data.
